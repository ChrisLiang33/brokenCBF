§1 — What ‖v‖ means (norm / length)

Q "what does ‖·‖ mean? what is linalg.norm?"

‖v‖ is just the LENGTH of vector v. The double-bar is math notation; np.linalg.norm(v) is the Python function. For a 2D vector:

v = (vx, vy) ‖v‖ = sqrt(vx² + vy²) (Pythagoras)

Example below: v = (3, 4) → ‖v‖ = √(9+16) = 5.

§2 — Identity matrix I₂

Q "what is the g(x) identity matrix, what does it look like?"

It's a 2×2 matrix that does NOTHING when you multiply by it — a math passthrough.

I₂ = [ 1 0 ] [ 0 1 ] [ 1 0 ] [ vx ] [ vx ] [ 0 1 ] [ vy ] = [ vy ] ← same vector back

Python: np.eye(2) @ v == v for any v.

So when we say g(x) = I₂ in the dynamics ẋ = f(x) + g(x)·u, we're saying "your input is the velocity, no transformation." That's true for the holonomic point-mass model used in the CBF math (the locomotion network handles the messy joint-level dynamics separately).

If your robot were a car (input = steering angle, not velocity), g(x) would be a non-trivial matrix encoding "how does steering map to position rate?" — and L_g h would NOT equal ∇h.

§3 — Dot product a · b

Two vectors in, one number out. The ONLY thing you really need to remember is the SIGN:

a · b = ax·bx + ay·by = ‖a‖·‖b‖·cos(θ) positive → a, b roughly same way (angle < 90°) zero → perpendicular (angle = 90°) negative → roughly opposite (angle > 90°)

§4 — h(x): the safety scalar

h(x) is the safety function. For one circular obstacle:

h(p_rob) = ‖p_rob − p_obs‖ − (R_rob + R_obs) └─── distance ───┘ └── combined radius ──┘ h > 0 → safe (gap exists) h = 0 → touching boundary h < 0 → collision (penetrating)

Your real _compute_h blends multiple obstacles via exponential smoothing, but the idea is the same: bigger h = farther from danger.

§5 — Why L_g h ends up being (p_rob − p_obs) / ‖p_rob − p_obs‖

Q "i thought L_g h = ∂h/∂x · g(x)"

Yes — and after you DO that math, it simplifies:

h = ‖p_rob − p_obs‖ − R let δ = p_rob − p_obs (the "delta" vector) h = ‖δ‖ − R (R is constant, drops out of gradient) ∇h = ∂h/∂p_rob = ∂‖δ‖/∂p_rob The calculus identity for "gradient of a vector's length": ∂‖δ‖/∂δ = δ / ‖δ‖ (this is just an unit vector along δ) ∂δ/∂p_rob = I₂ (since δ = p_rob − p_obs) Chain rule: ∇h = (δ / ‖δ‖) · I₂ = δ / ‖δ‖ = (p_rob − p_obs) / ‖p_rob − p_obs‖ Then: L_g h = ∇h · g(x) = (δ/‖δ‖) · I₂ = δ/‖δ‖ ← the identity matrix passes ∇h through unchanged.

So L_g h is a UNIT VECTOR (length 1) pointing FROM the obstacle TO the robot — the "escape direction." The reason it equals ∇h here is purely because g(x) = I₂.

§6 — Putting it all together: drag the sliders

Now feel it. Slack tells you whether the teleop command u_des already satisfies the CBF constraint:

slack = L_g h · u_des + α · h ───────────── ───── teleop term: margin term: is teleop how much safety heading already exists away? (h scaled by α) slack ≥ 0 → QP IDLE → u_safe = u_des → ∂u_safe/∂α = 0 slack < 0 → QP ACTIVE → u_safe ≠ u_des → α gradient flows

Move the robot, rotate u_des, change α. Watch slack. The "QP ACTIVE" zone is where any α-learning can happen.

What to play with (§6)

  • Push the robot far from obstacle (rx → max). h gets large, α·h dominates, slack stays positive regardless of u_des direction → QP IDLE everywhere → α has no gradient. This is most of your training.
  • Drop α to 0.1. The margin term shrinks. Now u_des direction starts to matter. Rotate u_des — you can flip QP between idle and active.
  • Crank α to 5.0 with a tight robot position. Even pointing u_des straight at the obstacle, slack stays positive. α "shielded" the constraint into uselessness.
  • Sweet spot for learning: small h (close obstacle), small-to-mid α, u_des roughly hostile. That's where the QP fires and α-gradient flows. Curriculum implication: spawn closer to obstacles.

§7 — The CBF as a projection (control space)

So far we've been in physical space (where the robot is). Now zoom into control space — the 2D plane where u_des and u_safe live (axes = u_x and u_y, the velocity components). This is where you actually see the CBF filter do its job.

Constraint: L_g h · u ≥ rhs where rhs = − α·h (Layer 1, your current setup) = − α(h − c) + φ‖L_g h‖² + a (Layer 4, full robust CBF) In u-space this is a STRAIGHT LINE perpendicular to L_g h. It cuts u-space into two halves: ALLOWED half-plane (green) : L_g h · u ≥ rhs → u_safe = u_des (QP idle) FORBIDDEN half-plane (red) : L_g h · u < rhs → u_safe = closest (QP active) point on the line

The "closest point" rule is just orthogonal projection: drop a perpendicular from u_des onto the boundary line. The projection direction is along −L_g h (the only direction that changes how much of u points along L_g h). In your code:

u_safe = u_des − L_g h · (slack / ‖L_g h‖²) where slack = L_g h · u_des − rhs

For this demo I fixed L_g h = (1, 0) (pointing in +u_x direction) so the constraint line is vertical at u_x = rhs. Drag the sliders below — watch the line move as α and h change, and watch u_safe snap onto the line whenever u_des crosses into the red zone.

What to play with (§7)

  • Increase α. The constraint line slides LEFT (rhs gets more negative). The allowed green zone grows. More u_des values are accepted as-is — fewer projections happen. This is the "α-shield" effect: bigger α makes the QP idle more often.
  • Decrease h toward 0. The line slides toward u_x = 0 — the constraint hugs the origin. Even small u_des with negative u_x gets projected. Close to obstacle = strict CBF.
  • Make h negative (collision). Line jumps to the right of origin. Now even u_des = 0 is forbidden — the CBF FORCES motion away from obstacle. This is the recovery behavior the math guarantees.
  • Rotate u_des by 180°. When u_des points along +L_g h (positive u_x), it's deep in the green zone and unaffected. When it points along −L_g h (toward obstacle), it gets projected. The CBF only filters the dangerous component.
  • Notice u_safe's y-component never changes. Only the u_x component (along L_g h) gets clipped. Tangential motion is preserved. This is why you can still walk past obstacles — the CBF doesn't kill perpendicular motion.
  • Connect to gradient. When the dot is in the green zone, sliding α doesn't move u_safe at all. This is the ∂u_safe/∂α = 0 fact you derived earlier, made visual.

§8 — How φ, a, c each shape the constraint (full robust CBF)

Layer 1 only uses α. The full 4-param robust CBF has three more terms, each shifting the constraint line for a different reason:

rhs = − α(h − c) + φ‖L_g h‖² + a α (class-K decay) : bigger α → line shifts LEFT → MORE permissive c (boundary correction) : bigger c → line shifts RIGHT → LESS permissive (treats h as if smaller — extra safety buffer) φ (actuation noise) : bigger φ → line shifts RIGHT → LESS permissive (extra margin against unmodeled dynamics) a (measurement bias) : bigger a → line shifts RIGHT → LESS permissive (extra margin against perception bias)

So three of the four parameters are "conservatism dials" (φ, a, c) — each one says "I think the world is worse than h reports, give me more margin." Only α trades off how aggressive the policy is. That's why each term in your layer-by-layer plan has its own paired DR axis: φ should adapt to actuation noise, a to perception bias, c to radius error. Without that DR, those slots have nothing to learn against.

§9 — The full CBF in physical space (multi-obstacle, animated)

Most realistic version: 3 obstacles, smoothed h(x) blending all of them, the full CBF filter running. Drag the robot with your mouse, set u_des direction with the slider, then hit Animate to watch the robot try to follow u_des while the CBF deflects around obstacles.

For N obstacles, h is a smoothed minimum (your code uses this exact form): h(p_rob) ≈ − τ · log( Σᵢ exp( −hᵢ / τ ) ) hᵢ = ‖p_rob − p_obs_i‖ − (R_rob + Rᵢ) Gradient becomes a weighted blend of individual escape directions: ∇h = Σᵢ wᵢ · (p_rob − p_obs_i) / ‖p_rob − p_obs_i‖ wᵢ ∝ exp(−hᵢ/τ) → closer obstacles get more weight (softmin) → wedged between two obstacles ⇒ ∇h points along their bisector

Dashed circles around each obstacle show the buffered radius (R_obs + R_rob) — that's the actual collision boundary the CBF protects.

drag robot with mouse · u_des stays constant during animation

What to play with (§9)

  • α=1.0, u_des pointing up, robot at lower-left → Animate. Watch the trajectory bend around the obstacles. The CBF deflected only when it had to. Reset, then try α=5.0 — same setup, different (less safe-in-clutter) path.
  • Wedge robot between two obstacles. Look at L_g h direction — it bisects the escape route. Both obstacles contribute via the softmin weights when their hᵢ are similar.
  • Open space ⇒ idle. Drag robot to a corner away from all obstacles. Status badge flips to QP IDLE — u_safe = u_des, no green arrow at all. This is most of your training data.
  • α gradient flows only when active. Drag near an obstacle until the badge says ACTIVE. Now slide α — the green arrow visibly rotates. Drag to open space, slide α — green arrow doesn't move (it equals u_des). That's the on/off α-gradient mechanism.
  • Implication for training data design: if you want stronger α-gradient signal, your environment needs more steps where the QP is active. Tighter obstacle density or spawn-near-obstacles is a curriculum lever to consider.

§10 — LHS field over physical space (where the QP fires)

Colored map of LHS = L_g h · u_des + α·h across the entire arena, for the α and u_des direction you set. Green = idle zone (constraint slack, QP doesn't fire). Red = active zone (constraint violated by u_des, QP must project). Black = inside obstacle.

This is the answer to "as observation changes, how does LHS change?" — every position in the world is one possible obs (h, L_g h depend on position), and the heatmap shows LHS at every one.

For each grid cell at world position (x, y): h(x, y), L_g h(x, y) computed from softmin over the same 3 obstacles LHS(x, y) = L_g h(x, y) · u_des + α · h(x, y) The boundary where LHS = 0 is the "QP firing line": cross from green into red and the projection turns on. Sweep the sliders to watch this boundary breathe.

What to play with (§10)

  • Default (α=1, u_des up). See the green-red boundary curl around each obstacle on the side the planner points toward. The thin red crescents are exactly where the QP fires.
  • Slide α from 0.1 to 5.0. Watch the red crescents SHRINK and eventually vanish. At α=5, almost nothing is red — the α-shield made the constraint never bind. This is why high-α policies act like raw teleop.
  • Slide α to 0.1. Red blooms outward — even far from obstacles, the constraint is touchy. Fixed-α=0.1 is "always cautious." It's why low-α can win on safety but lose on goal-reach.
  • Rotate u_des by 180°. The red crescents flip sides — they always sit on the obstacle side that u_des points TOWARD. The CBF is purely reactive to "where teleop is heading."
  • Imagine training data here. Robots spawn at random positions, walk for 800 steps. Most of the canvas is GREEN — this is where most of your training samples live. The α gradient is dead in green. Only the slim red region produces α-learning signal. This is what v3.0b/c tries to fix.
  • Connection to v3.0b. By putting h(x,y) and L_g h·u_des into the policy's observation, the policy can directly see "I'm at a green spot" or "I'm near the red boundary" and choose α accordingly. Without this obs, it has to learn it from the raw 8192-dim grid.
