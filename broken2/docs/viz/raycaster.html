RayCaster & the "one mesh path" problem

Two interactive scenes. Drag the obstacle in scene 1. Switch the RayCaster target in scene 2. Both demos illustrate what Isaac Lab's RayCaster sensor does and the specific limitation we hit.

Scene 1 — How RayCaster reads distances

A robot at the left fires 30 rays in a 240° arc. Each ray returns the distance to the first thing it hits. Drag the box to see the rays react.

ray hit (shows distance) ray miss (goes to infinity) robot (ray source) obstacle (drag me)

Key behavior: each ray stops at the first intersection. Rays can't pass through the box and continue. This matches real LiDAR physics — light doesn't go through opaque surfaces.

Scene 2 — The "one mesh path" problem

Three parallel envs, each with a robot and its own local obstacle. In Isaac Lab, RayCaster.mesh_prim_paths only accepts one path. Click a button below to pick which env's obstacle the RayCaster is configured to target.

ray hit (obstacle is "visible" to RayCaster) ray passes through (obstacle is "invisible")

Only the targeted env's obstacle appears in RayCaster's BVH — rays hitting it stop at the first intersection. For the other two envs, rays pass right through the obstacle as if it weren't there. The obstacles still exist physically (the robots will still collide with them in PhysX) — they're just invisible to the LiDAR sensor.

This is why we hit "only 4 envs actually see the obstacle" in Week 3 — with 4096 parallel envs and a single global obstacle, most robots never got useful rays. The fix was to disable LiDAR entirely for the teacher and feed ground-truth obstacle position directly into priv_obs.
